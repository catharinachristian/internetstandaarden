Techno

Techno is a genre of electronic dance music 'EDM' that is typically created for continuous DJ sets. The tempo of techno music usually ranges from 120 to 150 beats per minute (bpm), according to Marcello Perri. The main rhythm of techno is typically in common time (4/4) and is often characterized by a repetitive four on the floor beat. Artists in this genre use electronic instruments such as drum machines, sequencers, and synthesizers, as well as digital audio workstations. Drum machines like Roland's TR-808 and TR-909 from the 1980s are highly valued, and software imitations of these vintage instruments are popular.

In techno, the emphasis is placed on rhythm rather than other musical elements. Techno tracks progress primarily through the manipulation of timbral characteristics of synthesizer presets. Unlike other forms of EDM that are often created using synthesizer keyboards, techno does not adhere strictly to the harmonic practices of Western music. Instead, it often disregards traditional structures in favor of solely manipulating timbre. Another defining feature of techno music and its aesthetic is the openness to creative use of music production technology.

The term techno was first used in Germany in the early 1980s to describe a type of electronic music. After the release of the compilation Techno! The New Dance Sound of Detroit in the UK in 1988, the term became associated with a specific subgenre of EDM produced in Detroit. Detroit techno emerged from the fusion of synth-pop with African American styles like house, electro, and funk. It was also influenced by futuristic and science-fiction themes that reflected contemporary American society, with Alvin Toffler's book The Third Wave as a notable source of inspiration. The music created in the mid-to-late 1980s by artists like Juan Atkins, Derrick May, and Kevin Saunderson (known as The Belleville Three), along with Eddie Fowlkes, Blake Baxter, James Pennington, and others, is considered the first wave of techno from Detroit.

Following the success of house music in several European countries, techno gained popularity in the UK, Germany, Belgium, and the Netherlands. Regional variations of techno quickly developed in Europe, and by the early 1990s, subgenres such as acid, hardcore, bleep, ambient, and dub techno emerged. Music journalists and techno fans are generally careful in their use of the term, making a clear distinction between related but different styles, such as tech house and trance.

Club drugs, also known as rave drugs or party drugs, are recreational drugs commonly associated with discothèques in the 1970s and nightclubs, dance clubs, electronic dance music (EDM) parties, and raves from the 1980s to the present day. Unlike certain other drug classifications, such as opiates and benzodiazepines, which are determined based on pharmaceutical or chemical characteristics, club drugs are considered a "convenience category." This means that drugs are included in this category based on the locations where they are consumed or the activities the user engages in while under their influence. Generally, club drugs are most commonly used by adolescents and young adults.
